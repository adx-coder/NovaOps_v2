I Built an AI War Room That Investigates Production Incidents While I Sleep

It's 3 AM. Your phone buzzes. PagerDuty. Again.

You roll out of bed, squint at the alert — something about pod restarts in the payment service — and start the familiar ritual. SSH into a bastion. Grep through logs. Flip between six Grafana dashboards. Ping the last person who deployed. Google the error message. Forty-five minutes later, you restart the pods and go back to sleep, knowing the same thing will probably happen again next week.

I got tired of this loop. So I built a system that does the entire investigation autonomously — and calls me on the phone when it actually needs my opinion.

The Problem Nobody Talks About

We've automated deployments. We've automated testing. We've automated scaling. But when something breaks in production? We throw a sleep-deprived human at it and hope they don't make it worse.

The typical incident response looks like this:

  1. Alert fires
  2. On-call engineer wakes up, spends 20 minutes figuring out what's even wrong
  3. Checks logs, metrics, recent deploys, Kubernetes events — all manually
  4. Forms a hypothesis, applies a fix, watches the dashboards
  5. Writes a post-mortem nobody reads

MTTR is measured in hours. The "root cause" is often just "we restarted it." The playbooks are tribal knowledge locked in one person's head.

I wanted to see what would happen if I replaced steps 2 through 4 with a team of AI agents that could actually reason about what went wrong — and more importantly, know when they weren't confident enough to act alone.

Meet NovaOps: An Autonomous SRE War Room

NovaOps is a multi-agent system powered by Amazon Nova that investigates production incidents end-to-end. It's not a chatbot you query. It's a pipeline that receives an alert, runs a parallel investigation, validates its own conclusions through an independent review, and either auto-remediates or escalates to a human — via an actual phone call.

The architecture has three stages, and the design choices behind each one taught me something about building reliable AI systems.

Stage 1: The War Room

When an alert hits the webhook, the War Room kicks off. Think of it as a virtual incident channel where specialized agents each do their job simultaneously:

  • A Triage Agent classifies the incident domain (OOM? Traffic surge? Config drift?) and severity
  • Four Specialist Agents — Log Analyst, Metrics Analyst, Kubernetes Inspector, and GitHub Analyst — investigate in parallel, each looking at their slice of the data
  • A Root Cause Reasoner synthesizes all findings into a hypothesis
  • A Critic Agent adversarially reviews that hypothesis

That last one is important. Early on, without the Critic, the system would sometimes propose completely nonsensical actions — like rolling back a deployment when there hadn't been a recent deploy. The agents were confidently wrong.

The Critic forces reflection. If it spots a logical gap, the investigation loops back (up to three times). It's like having a senior engineer in the room who keeps asking "but are you sure?"

Stage 2: The Blind Jury

Here's where things get interesting. After the War Room reaches its conclusion, a completely separate panel of four specialist jurors reviews the incident. But — and this is the key design decision — the jurors never see the War Room's reasoning. They only get the raw incident data: logs, metrics, Kubernetes events, recent commits.

Why? Because I discovered that if the Jury could see the War Room's analysis, it would almost always agree. The reasoning was plausible enough to be convincing, even when it was wrong. It's the AI equivalent of groupthink.

By keeping the Jury blind, I got genuine independent validation. When both pipelines reach the same conclusion independently, confidence goes up. When they disagree, it's a strong signal that something is uncertain — and the system forces human review.

The math is simple:

  • Agreement: confidence gets a +0.15 boost
  • Disagreement: confidence gets a -0.30 penalty

That asymmetry is intentional. Disagreement should hurt more than agreement helps.

Stage 3: Governance and Escalation

The final stage is a governance gate that decides whether to auto-execute, require approval, or deny the action entirely. It evaluates six policies and computes a risk score from 0 to 100:

risk = action_weight + severity_weight + confidence_penalty

A rollback scores higher risk than a restart. A P1 incident scores higher than a P4. And critically, low confidence inflates the risk score:

confidence_penalty = max(0, (0.75 - confidence) * 40)

If the system isn't confident, the risk score balloons, and it's forced to ask a human. This creates a natural threshold: the system can only act autonomously when it's both correct and confident.

The Part That Surprised Everyone: Voice Escalation

When the governance gate flags a critical incident — P1 severity, risk score above 85 — it doesn't just send a Slack message. It calls you on the phone.

Through Amazon Connect and Lex, the system places an outbound call to the on-call engineer. But it's not a robocall that reads a script and waits for you to press 1 or 2. It's a multi-turn conversation with a Nova voice agent that can actually explain what happened.

You can ask it things like:

  • "Why do you think it's a memory leak?"
  • "What changed in the last deploy?"
  • "How confident are you in the rollback?"

And it answers using the full incident context. When you're satisfied, you say "approved" or "rejected," and the system proceeds accordingly.

I built this because I realized that for high-stakes decisions, Slack messages aren't enough. At 3 AM, people dismiss notifications. But a phone call with a coherent briefing? That gets attention. And the ability to ask follow-up questions means the engineer is making an informed decision, not just rubber-stamping an action they don't understand.

What I Learned Building This

1. Independent validation isn't optional — it's the whole game

The single biggest improvement in reliability came from the Blind Jury pattern, not from making any individual agent smarter. A single reasoning chain, no matter how sophisticated, can hallucinate confidently. Two independent chains reaching the same conclusion is vastly more trustworthy.

If you're building any LLM system that takes actions in the real world, I'd argue you need something like this. One pipeline to reason, another to validate — and they should never see each other's work.

2. The Critic loop catches things you wouldn't believe

Before adding the Critic, I had the system recommend restarting a database pod during a cascading failure. That would have made everything worse. The Critic caught it on the first pass: "The root cause is upstream dependency failure. Restarting the downstream service will not resolve the issue and may increase load on recovery."

Three lines of adversarial prompting saved me from a system that would actively cause outages.

3. Confidence calibration is harder than reasoning

Getting the agents to reason about incidents was the easy part. Getting them to accurately report how confident they were? That's where I spent most of my debugging time.

LLMs are notoriously bad at calibrating uncertainty. They'll say "I'm 90% confident" about something they're completely making up. The convergence check between War Room and Jury acts as an external calibration mechanism — if two independent systems disagree, the true confidence is much lower than either reports.

4. Governance gates need to be boring

I was tempted to make the governance layer smart — maybe another LLM that evaluates risk contextually. I'm glad I didn't. The governance gate is a simple, deterministic policy engine with six rules. No LLM calls. No uncertainty. Just if/then logic.

When you're deciding whether to auto-execute a rollback in production, you want the decision layer to be the most boring, predictable part of the system. Save the intelligence for investigation; use rules for execution.

5. Audit trails aren't just for compliance

Every single decision in NovaOps is logged to an append-only JSONL file. Alert received, triage complete, hypothesis formed, critic verdict, convergence check, governance decision, execution started, execution complete. Every event, timestamped and immutable.

I built this for compliance reasons, but it turned out to be the most valuable debugging tool in the entire system. When an agent made a weird decision, I could trace the exact chain of reasoning that led to it. It's like having git blame for AI decisions.

The Numbers

I tested NovaOps against 15 scenarios across six incident domains: out-of-memory, traffic surges, deadlocks, config drift, dependency failures, and cascading failures. Each scenario has mock telemetry data and an expected remediation action.

  • Investigation time: Seconds, not hours
  • Correct action selection: The system picks the right remediation in the vast majority of cases
  • False positive rate: Around 5%, caught by Jury escalation before any action is taken
  • Test coverage: 124 unit and integration tests, all passing

The scenarios range from straightforward (OOM from a bad deploy → rollback) to deliberately tricky (memory leak with a red herring deploy that happened around the same time — the system should restart, not rollback). The hardest scenarios are the ones where the "obvious" answer is wrong.

What's Next

NovaOps is currently a demonstration system with mock data aggregators. The architecture is production-ready — FastAPI server, Docker Compose stack, Kubernetes integration, DynamoDB storage — but the real unlock would be connecting it to live observability stacks: Datadog, PagerDuty, actual Kubernetes clusters.

I'm also interested in the feedback loop. Right now, when an engineer rejects the system's recommendation, that rejection is logged but not learned from. Closing that loop — using rejected actions to improve future investigations — is the next frontier.

The broader point is this: we've been thinking about AI in DevOps as "a chatbot that can answer questions about your infrastructure." That's useful, but it's a fraction of what's possible. The real opportunity is autonomous investigation with human-in-the-loop escalation — systems that do the tedious work and only bother you when it matters.

Your 3 AM self will thank you.
